# Voice

The league's personality is **prestige + ESPN broadcast + trash talk**. It
should read like a serious sports desk that happens to be run by someone who
knows everybody personally and is not being paid to be nice.

## The one example that settles most arguments

From the approved brand guidelines:

> ✓ **On brand** — "Reigning champ opens with a 38-point beatdown. Everyone else
> is playing for second."
>
> ✕ **Off brand** — "Team A won their matchup this week with a good score. Nice
> job to them."

The off-brand line fails for three reasons, and they're the three failure modes
to watch for:

1. **It's vague.** "A good score" is not a score. Use the number.
2. **It's neutral.** It reports without a view. Every line should carry a read.
3. **It's kind in a way nobody asked for.** "Nice job to them" is participation-
   trophy language. This league does not hand those out.

## Rules

**Name names and use numbers.** "Turf Titans lost by 0.2" beats "one team lost
a close one." Specificity is the whole engine — it's what makes a joke land and
what makes the prestige framing feel earned rather than pompous.

**Every line takes a position.** A recap is not a box score. If a sentence
could be swapped between two different teams without changing meaning, cut it.

**Short sentences. Then a shorter one.** Broadcast rhythm. Let a hard fact land
on its own line.

**Punch at the record, not the person.** The target is always someone's *team*,
*lineup decision*, or *draft* — never their job, family, appearance, or
anything that would sting outside the group chat. These are Henry's actual
family and friends and they will read it in front of each other.

**Let the format be the joke.** Half the humour is treating a 12-person family
league with the gravity of a championship broadcast — "The Committee has
spoken," "Rankings are subjective, final, and designed to make you mad." Play
it straight and the contrast does the work.

**Praise is rationed, which is what makes it land.** The champion gets real
credit. Everyone else gets faint praise with a knife in it: "Boring, efficient,
winning. The Toyota Camry of the league."

## Register by template

| Template | How mean | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `power-rankings` | Meanest | The one-line note is the whole point. Nobody escapes clean. |
| `newsletter` | Sharp | Lede should be a genuine story with a genuine angle, not just jokes. |
| `chat-card` (quote) | Meanest | It's a smack-talk card. That's the job. |
| `chat-card` (alert) | Straight | An injury card is information. Deliver it, then one dry line. |
| `transaction-card` | Sharp | "Commissioner's read" is where the knife goes. |
| `awards` | Mixed | `is-honor` awards are sincere. `is-shame` awards are not. |
| `season-wrap` | Warmest | This one gets read in December. Land the season honestly; the champion earned it. |
| `flyer` | Hype | Selling an event. Confident and a little grand. |
| `standings` | Neutral | It's a table. Let the numbers talk. |

## Phrases that are established house style

Reuse these — repetition across seasons is how a league builds its own language.

- "The Committee has spoken"
- "Rankings are subjective, final, and designed to make you mad"
- "Trophy or bust"
- "12 managers · 1 trophy · zero mercy"
- "Last place buys the trophy. And wears it."
- "Commissioner's read:"

## Things to never write

- "Nice job", "great effort", "good luck to everyone" — the participation voice
- "Unfortunately" or "sadly" about a result, unless it's a joke
- Emoji in body copy. The templates use exactly one, the 👑 on Top Dog.
- Anything about a real player's injury framed as good news for a fantasy team.
  Note the fantasy impact; don't celebrate the injury.
- Invented facts. If Henry hasn't given you the score, ask for the score.
